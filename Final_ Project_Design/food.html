Spice of Life

Why is spice used? Spices are dried plant parts, such as bark, buds, fruit, roots, seeds, or stems, that have a strong flavor. Chefs are aware that spices enhance flavor and enhance the world's palate. Without the flavor of cumin in Texas chili, golden turmeric in chicken tikka masala, or cinnamon in apple pie, where would we be? Spices release their aromatic oils when they are toasted whole over medium heat in a dry skillet, so toss them around for a few minutes. Allow them to cool down before grinding. I frequently toast pre-ground spices, like cinnamon and turmeric, because the heat enhances their flavor. Simply take care not to burn them. The best way to incorporate flavor into a rice or vegetable dish is to cook the dried herb or ground spice in butter or oil before adding the main ingredients. Because of the heat and fat, the spice can develop and the flavor will permeate the entire dish more fully. Adding spices and dried herbs early in the cooking process is preferable to doing so later. In the end, fresh herbs are added. Just be sure to add the dried herbs early in the cooking process to give the dish time to absorb their flavor. Knowing what and how to add spices to a dish can help you become a better chef because they add something extra to the food.

Food Decor

It is crucial to not only cook mouthwatering food but to adorn it as well, making it visually appealing as well as tasty. Dishes should be presented and unfolded with great care. When presented correctly, a beautifully decorated salad attracts more attention than its "modest neighbor". Incorporate ornamental elements: although they aren't strictly ornamental, their function is more aesthetic than organoleptic, and they will contribute taste and texture to the meal. Salads, seeds, herbs, and edible flowers are what frequently accompany and adorn our food. Since people eat with their eyes, food presentation needs to be visually appealing in order to persuade some people to try new foods or to eat something they wouldn't normally.
